Ethnicity facts and figures background

The Prime Minister announced the Race Disparity Audit in August 2016 to publish government data on disparities in treatment and outcomes in public services for all races.

This page provides a brief overview of the way Government undertook the project, looking at where it came from, what was done and how it was carried out.

Project phases

1. Identification

The initial review required all government departments to identify what information on race and ethnicity they held on UK public services. Devolved administrations were asked to participate and share their data in order to provide a UK-wide picture. This means that:

  • local authority data is not included unless it is also available from a central department (e.g. the Department for Education provides school data in England at a local level)
  • Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland data is included in areas that are not devolved (e.g. the Home Office reports on victims of crime across the UK), and are continuing discussions with devolved government to to include more of their data in the future

2. Prioritisation

The identification stage identified a vast amount of data which it was clear could not all be collated and processed within a short timeframe. So the team, in consultation with departments and based on user insights, agreed criteria to shorten the list for the first release. All other remaining datasets will be continually added after launch . This approach will improve ethnicity data collection and reporting over time, as well as making more content available. The prioritisation criteria signed off by Ministers were:

  • whether the dataset relates to a Government priority
  • whether it has been identified by future users of the platform as a priority for them
  • “manageability” - how much work is involved in preparing the data for publication

3. Collection

Based on Cabinet Office user research, data expertise and existing good practices from ONS publishing, a format for collecting content from departments for each measure was established.

The data held by different departments varies greatly in size, consistency and quality. There is limited standardisation and categorisation of the different ethnicities can vary greatly depending on how data was collected, when and by whom. A common format is needed to ensure trust, quality and value across the content presented on the website. Each measure, which relates to an individual web page, is built of a combination of 2 file templates:

  • data format file - this data is used to populate charts and tables
  • commentary - this provides the content describing the facts and figures displayed, as well as caveats, technical details and how the data was collected

Government departments returned these files for each of the prioritised measures which then went through additional checks to ensure quality and consistency before being uploaded to the site.

4. Publishing

All of the collected data has been made available on a website on GOV.UK. The Ethnicity facts and figures website was developed based on extensive user research and the government’s Digital Service Standard. This work has been undertaken in 3 stages:

  • discovery: understand the project scope and identify users
  • alpha: prototype the website, refining user needs and identifying the solutions to buy
  • beta: build the platform and continue testing extensively with users

The website was reviewed by experts through service assessments to ensure it met the required standards, and was signed off by the Minister for the Cabinet Office before launch. This rigorous approach checks whether a service is good enough for public use and meets user needs.

Way the programme was carried out

Whilst such a project has not been undertaken before, the principles are well established through a range of areas work including both the UK Statistical Authority code and Government Digital Service’s Digital Service Standard.

The Code of Practice for Official Statistics promotes the production and dissemination of official statistics that inform decision making, and supports the continuous improvement of those statistics. It is a concise and specific statement that requires sound judgement and interpretation.

The Digital Service Standard is a set of 18 criteria to help government create and run good digital services. All public facing transactional services must meet the standard. It’s used by departments and the Government Digital Service to check whether a service is good enough for public use. The first of the 18 criteria is “Understand user needs”.

Consultation and engagement

In order to benefit from leading practice, expertise, rigorous public consultation and testing, the project has engaged and co-designed deeply throughout the programme.

As an example of this building on existing work, we referenced the measurement framework used by the Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC). The EHRC framework measures and monitors progress on equality and human rights in England, Scotland and Wales and consists of a number of domains, indicators and measures. The Ethnicity facts and figures framework has a parallel structure in the form of topics, sub-topics and measures:

  • topics reflect the key areas within the public sector that are important to people
  • subtopics group together similar subject areas within a topic
  • measures capture specific facts and figures on a subject e.g. victims of crime, unemployment

The website is structured around this framework and has been thoroughly tested to check that users can navigate easily and find the content that they are looking for.

Understanding user needs

Cabinet Office have spoken to hundreds of users since the project started, to help understand who the users are, what their different needs are, how those might already be met elsewhere, and what problems they had that the website could solve. The different groups of users included:

  • members of the public
  • government policy officials and analysts
  • press and media
  • non-governmental organisations
  • academics
  • public service managers e.g. headteachers, job centre managers

Their needs vary a great deal as does their comprehension of statistical data. The content has to be presented in a clear and meaningful way to non-experts in statistics and data. It was also needed to ensure it was accessible so that visually or manually impaired people could use the service. Users with more expertise in statistics and data manipulation needed access to the data and richer background information to give context on how it was collected and analysed.

Users came from a variety of ethnic backgrounds, locations and demographic profiles to reflect the diversity of the website’s user base.

Future changes

More measures will be added to the website over time. This will consist of new measures as well as updates over time to those already published.

The website will continue to be updated based on user feedback and what we learn after launch. Any feedback or queries should be sent to ethnicity@cabinetoffice.gov.uk.
